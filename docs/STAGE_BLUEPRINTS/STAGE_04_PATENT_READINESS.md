# Stage 4: Patent Readiness

---

## Stage Overview

**Purpose:**
Assess whether the invention has patentable elements, identify relevant prior art, and prepare the inventor for an informed decision about intellectual property protection before engaging a patent attorney.

**Why this stage exists:**
Filing a patent prematurely — before understanding the prior art landscape — wastes thousands of dollars and can result in a patent that offers no meaningful protection. Filing too late risks losing priority to a competitor. Most inventors have no framework for making this decision intelligently. They either file immediately because "that's what you do," or they defer indefinitely because the process feels opaque and expensive. Atlas provides a middle path: a structured prior art search, a patentability assessment, and a clear recommendation on whether and when to engage a patent attorney. This stage does not replace legal counsel — it makes the inventor's first meeting with an IP attorney dramatically more productive and efficient.

**Primary objective:**
Produce a Patent Readiness Report that tells the inventor whether their invention likely has patentable elements, what the strongest claims would be, and what prior art they need to understand before filing.

**Success definition:**
Atlas considers this stage successful when the inventor has conducted a structured prior art search, articulated their invention's novel elements as specific claim-like statements, and documented a clear IP strategy decision — whether to proceed to filing, defer, or pursue trade secret protection — with the reasoning recorded.

---

## Inventor Goals

- Understand whether the invention has patentable elements in plain terms — not legal language
- Learn how to search for prior art and evaluate whether a found patent actually affects their claims
- Articulate the novel aspects of the invention clearly enough to brief an IP attorney
- Make an informed, documented decision about IP strategy before spending money on legal fees
- Leave this stage with a document that makes the attorney engagement faster and more efficient

---

## Atlas Responsibilities

**How Atlas guides the inventor:**
Atlas explains the four requirements for patentability in plain language — novel, non-obvious, useful, and sufficiently described — with concrete examples that make each concept tangible. It then guides the inventor through a structured prior art self-search: which databases to use, which keywords to search, how to read a patent abstract, and how to evaluate whether a cited patent actually anticipates their claims. Atlas holds the inventor's hand through what is, for most first-time inventors, a completely unfamiliar process.

**What Atlas analyzes:**
- The inventor's description of novel elements — for specificity, mechanism clarity, and claim-like structure
- Prior art findings the inventor provides — for relevance, claim overlap, and what gaps they reveal
- The competitive products from Stage 3 — as potential prior art references and as evidence of what the market already recognizes as distinct
- The IP strategy options relative to the inventor's timeline, budget, and market context

**What Atlas recommends:**
- Specific search keywords and databases for prior art research
- Which aspects of the invention are most likely patentable and why
- A prioritized list of prior art references to review before engaging an attorney
- A next action: file provisional immediately, conduct deeper search first, engage attorney now, or consider trade secret/first-mover strategy instead

**What Atlas tracks:**
- Number of prior art searches conducted and databases used
- Whether the inventor has reviewed the top 3 most relevant references
- Whether novel elements have been articulated as claim-like statements
- Whether an IP strategy decision has been documented

---

## Inputs

- Idea Brief from Stage 1 (the invention's mechanism and novel elements)
- Market Research Summary from Stage 3 (competitive products — which may represent prior art)
- Any patents or published applications the inventor is already aware of
- The inventor's description of the novel elements they believe differentiate their invention
- The inventor's IP strategy intentions: utility patent, design patent, trade secret, provisional, or none
- The inventor's IP timeline and budget constraints

---

## Atlas Guidance

Atlas opens the Patent Readiness stage by reframing what this work is for: not to become an amateur patent attorney, but to become an informed client. The inventor's job is to understand the landscape well enough to have a productive conversation with a professional — and to avoid the most common and costly mistake: spending money on IP without knowing what they're protecting or why.

Atlas introduces the four patentability requirements with accessible examples drawn from everyday inventions. It then explains what prior art means in practice: not "someone thought of it" but "someone publicly disclosed it before your filing date." This distinction matters, and Atlas makes it concrete.

The prior art search is guided step by step. Atlas provides specific search queries tailored to the invention's mechanism and domain. It explains how to read a patent claim — the most important part of any patent — and how to evaluate whether a claim in a found patent actually covers the inventor's specific implementation or merely adjacent territory. The goal is not to find zero prior art; the goal is to understand exactly what prior art exists and how it shapes the scope of what can be claimed.

As the inventor documents findings, Atlas helps them synthesize: "Given what you found, the most defensible novel element appears to be [specific mechanism]. The prior art shows this hasn't been done this way before." Atlas drafts the Strongest Claim — a plain-language articulation of the most defensible novel aspect.

The stage concludes with Atlas drafting the Patent Readiness Report and presenting an IP Strategy Recommendation. The inventor reviews both and documents their decision. If they decide to engage a patent attorney, Atlas prepares the IP Brief for that handoff.

- Novel element articulation — helping the inventor describe their invention in claim-like language
- Patentability assessment across novelty, non-obviousness, and utility dimensions

- Patent Readiness Report drafting
- IP Brief preparation for attorney engagement

## Readiness Evaluation

Atlas evaluates the inventor's readiness using three states only. No numeric scores.

**Not Ready:**
- No prior art searches have been conducted
- The inventor has not yet articulated what is novel about their invention beyond a general description
- No IP strategy decision has been considered or documented
- The Patent Readiness Report has not been drafted

**Getting There:**
- Prior art searches have been started but fewer than 5 searches completed, or only one database used
- Novel elements have been described but not yet structured as claim-like statements
- Top prior art references have been identified but not yet reviewed and summarized
- An IP strategy is being considered but no decision has been documented

**Ready to Move Forward:**
- At least 5 patent searches conducted using at least one of: USPTO, Google Patents, or Espacenet
- Top 3 most relevant prior art references reviewed and summarized with a relevance note
- Novel elements articulated as at least 3 specific claim-like statements
- IP strategy decision documented — proceed to filing, file provisional, defer, or pursue trade secret
- Patent Readiness Report reviewed and confirmed by the inventor

---

## Completion Criteria

- At least 5 patent searches conducted using USPTO, Google Patents, or Espacenet
- Top 3 most relevant prior art references reviewed, summarized, and committed to the Prior Art Summary
- Novel elements articulated as at least 3 claim-like statements
- Strongest Claim Draft completed
- IP strategy decision documented with reasoning
- Patent Readiness Report reviewed and confirmed by the inventor

---

## Handoff

The following information moves from Stage 4 into Stage 5 (Product Design) and forward to Stage 9 (Intellectual Property):

- **Patent Readiness Report** — the complete IP assessment; referenced throughout all remaining stages
- **Prior Art Summary** — carries forward to Stage 9 as the primary research document for the attorney engagement; the attorney builds on this work rather than starting from scratch
- **Novel Element Statements** — feed directly into Stage 5 (Product Design) as design constraints: the inventor knows which mechanisms to protect and can design around prior art intentionally
- **IP Strategy Recommendation** — establishes the timing framework for Stage 9; if a provisional filing is recommended before design finalization, it becomes a near-term priority
- **Strongest Claim Draft** — referenced in Stage 9 when structuring the attorney briefing
- **IP strategy decision and timing** — informs the sequencing of Stage 9 relative to Stage 5 and Stage 6

No prior art findings are discarded. The Prior Art Summary is a living reference document updated in Stage 9.

- **IP attorney marketplace** — allow inventors to book a consultation directly from the Patent Readiness Report, with the report pre-shared to the attorney before the meeting
