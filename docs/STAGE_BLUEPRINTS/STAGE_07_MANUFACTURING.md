# Stage 7: Manufacturing

---

## Stage Overview

**Purpose:**
Identify and qualify a manufacturing partner, establish production economics, and create a viable, documented path from prototype to manufactured product.

**Why this stage exists:**
Manufacturing is where most hardware inventors hit a wall. They have a validated prototype. They know they need to get it made. But they don't know how to find a manufacturer, what questions to ask, what a realistic unit cost looks like at different volumes, or how to structure a manufacturing relationship that protects their interests. They approach manufacturers as naive buyers and get treated accordingly. Atlas changes that: it gives inventors the vocabulary, the framework, and the documentation to engage manufacturers as informed, prepared partners rather than first-timers without leverage.

**Primary objective:**
Select a qualified manufacturing partner, establish unit economics across at least two production volumes, and produce a documented manufacturing plan ready to execute.

**Success definition:**
Atlas considers this stage successful when the inventor has received at least two manufacturer quotes against a complete Request for Quote, selected a manufacturing partner with documented rationale, and produced a Unit Economics Model that makes the business math clear at the initial and scale production volumes.

---

## Inventor Goals

- Understand the manufacturing landscape for their product category — domestic vs. overseas, contract manufacturer vs. OEM vs. ODM
- Produce a professional Request for Quote that manufacturers take seriously
- Receive and evaluate multiple quotes against a consistent framework
- Establish what the product will actually cost to make at different volumes
- Select a manufacturing partner with confidence — not by defaulting to the cheapest option
- Understand what a manufacturing agreement must contain to protect the invention

---

## Atlas Responsibilities

**How Atlas guides the inventor:**
Atlas explains the manufacturing decision framework in plain language — the different types of manufacturing relationships, the trade-offs between domestic and overseas production, and what certifications and standards a responsible manufacturer should hold. It then builds a manufacturer-ready Request for Quote from the design specification, guides the inventor through a structured evaluation of quotes received, and calculates unit economics at multiple production volumes. Atlas ensures the inventor understands the financial model before placing any order.

**What Atlas analyzes:**
- The Product Design Specification — for the manufacturing complexity implications of design choices
- The inventor's target unit cost and production volume expectations
- Manufacturer quotes received — for completeness, comparability, and red flags
- Unit economics across multiple production volumes — materials, labor, tooling amortization, overhead, and logistics
- Manufacturing agreement terms — for coverage of the most critical protective provisions

**What Atlas recommends:**
- The most appropriate manufacturing model for the product's category, complexity, and the inventor's scale targets
- Specific manufacturer evaluation criteria and disqualifying red flags
- A manufacturer selection with documented reasoning
- An initial production order quantity consistent with the sales forecast and cash position
- Minimum contract terms that must be included in any manufacturing agreement

**What Atlas tracks:**
- Number of manufacturers contacted and quotes received
- Unit economics completeness across at least two production volumes
- Whether a manufacturing partner has been selected and documented
- Whether manufacturing agreement terms have been reviewed against the protective provisions checklist

---

## Inputs

- Finalized Product Design Specification from Stage 6 (the prototype-validated design document manufacturers quote against)
- Prototype Test Report from Stage 6 (performance requirements for the manufactured product)
- Materials and components list from the design specification
- Target unit cost — from the inventor's estimate or from early Stage 10 (Pricing) work if done in parallel
- Target production volumes for the initial run and for scale
- Geographic preferences: domestic manufacturing, overseas, or both considered
- Any existing manufacturer relationships the inventor has

---

## Atlas Guidance

Atlas opens Stage 7 by helping the inventor understand the manufacturing landscape for their product category — not generically, but specifically. The questions that matter for a consumer electronics product are different from those for a soft goods accessory or an injection-molded consumer product. Atlas tailors its guidance to the product type.

Atlas then builds the Request for Quote — a structured document that translates the inventor's design specification into a manufacturer-facing format. The RFQ includes technical specifications, materials and tolerances, testing and quality standards, target quantities, and delivery requirements. Sending a complete, professional RFQ is how the inventor signals competence to a manufacturer and gets serious quotes in return.

Atlas guides the inventor through contacting at least 3 manufacturers — explaining how to find qualified manufacturers in the relevant category, how to make initial contact professionally, and what questions to ask before committing to the RFQ process. When quotes come back, Atlas provides a structured evaluation framework: unit cost at requested volumes, tooling cost, payment terms, lead time, minimum order quantity, quality certifications, and communication responsiveness.

The Unit Economics Model is built step by step: materials cost, labor, overhead, tooling amortization at the initial volume, logistics, and margin. Atlas presents the model at three production volumes so the inventor can see how unit economics improve with scale — and what volume they need to reach target margins.

For contract review, Atlas walks through the provisions that a manufacturing agreement must include: IP ownership and assignment, quality standards and defect remediation, delivery terms and penalties, payment terms, exclusivity provisions (or the absence of them), and NDA coverage. Atlas is not providing legal advice — it is preparing the inventor to have an informed conversation with a lawyer or to recognize a dangerous contract when they see one.

## Readiness Evaluation

Atlas evaluates the inventor's readiness using three states only. No numeric scores.

**Not Ready:**
- The RFQ document has not been completed
- No manufacturers have been contacted
- Unit economics have not been calculated at any production volume
- No manufacturing partner selection has been made or is in progress

**Getting There:**
- RFQ is complete and has been sent to at least one manufacturer
- At least one quote has been received but a second quote is still pending
- Unit economics are being calculated but are not complete across two volumes
- A manufacturer is under evaluation but no selection has been documented

**Ready to Move Forward:**
- RFQ document is complete and manufacturer-ready
- At least 3 manufacturers contacted and at least 2 quotes received
- Unit Economics Model is complete for at least two production volumes
- A manufacturing partner has been selected and the selection is documented with rationale
- Manufacturing agreement terms have been reviewed against the protective provisions checklist
- Initial production order quantity is defined
- Manufacturing Readiness Assessment is complete

---

## Completion Criteria

- RFQ document is complete and has been sent to at least 3 manufacturers
- At least 2 manufacturer quotes have been received and evaluated against the scorecard
- Unit Economics Model is complete for at least two production volumes
- A manufacturing partner has been selected with documented rationale
- Manufacturing agreement terms have been reviewed against Atlas's checklist
- Initial production order quantity is defined and consistent with the sales forecast
- Manufacturing Readiness Assessment is complete

---

## Handoff

The following information moves from Stage 7 into Stage 8 (Branding) and forward to Stages 10, 13, and 14:

- **Unit Economics Model** — the primary cost input to Stage 10 (Pricing); the cost-per-unit at each production volume is the foundation of the entire pricing analysis
- **Manufacturing Plan** — the selected manufacturer and production timeline feed Stage 14 (Launch) for logistics planning and inventory readiness
- **Manufacturing agreement review findings** — any IP-related provisions in the manufacturing agreement are flagged for Stage 9 (Intellectual Property) to ensure they are structured correctly
- **Initial production order quantity and timeline** — informs Stage 14 launch date planning and Stage 13 funding use of proceeds
- **Unit Economics Model** — feeds Stage 13 (Funding) as the cost foundation of the financial model in the investor pitch
- **Manufacturer contact record** — the manufacturer relationships established here are live assets carried throughout remaining stages
