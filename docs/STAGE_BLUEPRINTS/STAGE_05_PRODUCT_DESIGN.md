# Stage 5: Product Design

---

## Stage Overview

**Purpose:**
Transform the validated concept into a defined product specification — form, function, materials, and user experience — ready for prototyping.

**Why this stage exists:**
The gap between "I have an idea" and "I have a product" is bridged at this stage. Product Design is where the invention stops being abstract and becomes concrete: what does it look like, how does a user interact with it, what are its physical or digital specifications, and how does every design decision serve the target customer? Inventors who skip structured design work end up with prototypes that need to be rebuilt from scratch, because they discover in physical form that assumptions made in their head were wrong. Those rebuilds are expensive — in time, money, and momentum. Atlas guides the inventor through design decisions systematically, surfacing trade-offs before they become costly mistakes.

**Primary objective:**
Produce a Product Design Specification detailed enough for a prototyper, industrial designer, or engineering firm to begin work without needing to ask foundational questions.

**Success definition:**
Atlas considers this stage successful when the Product Design Specification is complete enough to hand to a skilled prototyper — and that prototyper can begin work without coming back with questions about what the product is supposed to do, who it's for, or what materials it should use.

---

## Inventor Goals

- Define what the product looks like, how it works, and how a user interacts with it
- Make deliberate design decisions — not defaults — and understand the trade-offs behind each one
- Identify which design decisions require specialist expertise and plan for those engagements
- Document the design with enough precision that the prototype can be built from the specification alone
- Arrive at the prototyping stage with clarity, not ambiguity

---

## Atlas Responsibilities

**How Atlas guides the inventor:**
Atlas structures design work into three layers — Functional, Physical, and User Experience — and walks the inventor through each one sequentially. At each decision point, Atlas presents structured options rather than open-ended questions. It surfaces the manufacturing implications of design choices in real time, connecting design decisions to their downstream cost and complexity consequences before the inventor commits. Atlas identifies when a decision requires professional expertise — materials engineering, electrical design, regulatory compliance — and flags those for specialist engagement rather than letting the inventor guess.

**What Atlas analyzes:**
- The Idea Brief and Patent Readiness Report — for which mechanisms must be precisely specified vs. where flexibility exists
- Customer Segment Profiles from Stage 3 — for how the target user will interact with the product in context
- The inventor's stated constraints: price point, materials preferences, size requirements, and regulatory environment
- Each design decision for its manufacturing implications — cost, minimum order quantities, tooling, and lead time

**What Atlas recommends:**
- Design options at each decision point, with the trade-offs made explicit
- Which design decisions to resolve now vs. which to defer to prototyping
- Specific specialist roles or firms to engage based on the design's technical complexity
- A design approach that is manufacturable, not just conceptually coherent

**What Atlas tracks:**
- Design specification completeness across all three layers: Functional, Physical, and User Experience
- Open design decisions that must be resolved before the prototyper can begin
- Design decisions that require specialist expertise
- Whether the specification is consistent with the Patent Readiness Report's novel elements

---

## Inputs

- Idea Brief from Stage 1 (the core mechanism and function)
- Customer Segment Profiles from Stage 3 (who uses this product, in what context, and with what expectations)
- Patent Readiness Report from Stage 4 (novel elements to protect, design constraints arising from prior art)
- The inventor's own sketches, reference images, or inspiration products
- Constraints the inventor has already identified: target retail price, materials preferences, size requirements, regulatory environment (FDA, CE marking, FCC, etc.)
- Any manufacturing context the inventor already has: preferred production geography, access to specific manufacturing processes

---

## Atlas Guidance

Atlas opens Stage 5 by orienting the inventor to the three-layer design framework: what the product does (Functional), what it is (Physical), and how it feels to use (User Experience). It explains that each layer must be defined before prototyping begins — not because design must be perfect, but because an underdefined specification produces an undirected prototype that answers the wrong questions.

Atlas works through each layer with structured, binary or constrained-option questions rather than open prompts. "Should this product prioritize durability or portability? These are often in tension at this price point, and optimizing for both usually means doing neither well." The inventor makes explicit choices; Atlas records them and their rationale in the Design Decision Log.

When a design decision has manufacturing implications, Atlas surfaces them immediately and concisely. Not as a warning or a barrier — as information: "Choosing injection-molded ABS for the housing implies tooling costs of $3,000–$8,000 and a minimum order of approximately 1,000 units. Is that consistent with your initial production scale target?" This lets the inventor make connected decisions rather than designing in isolation from their business model.

Atlas identifies points in the specification where professional expertise is required — structural engineering, electronic circuit design, food-contact material certification, medical device regulatory pathway — and documents these as Specialist Engagement Flags. Atlas is clear: it can help the inventor frame the design question, but it cannot replace the specialist's judgment.

The stage concludes with Atlas presenting the complete Product Design Specification, a Design Decision Log of every major choice and its rationale, and a prioritized list of Open Design Questions — decisions the prototyper will need to resolve in physical form.

## AI Tasks

- Functional specification structuring — translating the invention concept into defined functions with success criteria
- Physical specification guidance — prompting decisions on form factor, materials, dimensions, and weight
- User Experience specification — mapping the primary user journey from first encounter through ongoing use
- Manufacturing implication analysis — real-time consequence surfacing for design decisions
- Design Decision Log generation — recording every major decision with alternatives considered and rationale
- Design completeness evaluation — identifying gaps against a complete specification standard
- Open Design Questions list generation — prioritized by what must be resolved before prototyping vs. what can be resolved during
- Specialist Engagement Flag identification — recognizing when professional expertise is required

## Outputs

- **Product Design Specification** — the master design document covering function, form, materials, and user experience
- **Design Decision Log** — every key design decision, the alternatives considered, and the reasoning for the chosen path
- **Open Design Questions** — a prioritized list of decisions not yet resolved, with a recommended resolution approach for each
- **Specialist Engagement Recommendations** — specific professional roles or firms to engage based on the design's technical requirements

## Documents Created

- **Product Design Specification** — the master design document; all prototype work and manufacturing conversations reference this as the single source of truth for what the product is
- **Design Decision Log** — the audit trail of design reasoning; referenced when the invention evolves and design decisions need to be revisited
- **Open Design Questions** — the handoff checklist for the prototyper; this document becomes the prototype's testing agenda

## Readiness Evaluation

Atlas evaluates the inventor's readiness using three states only. No numeric scores.

**Not Ready:**
- Functional specification is incomplete — core product functions have not been defined with success criteria
- Physical specification is missing: form factor, materials, or dimensions have not been addressed
- User experience specification has not been started
- No visual reference (sketch, photo, wireframe) has been attached
- Multiple critical design decisions remain undocumented or unresolved

**Getting There:**
- Functional specification is mostly complete but some functions lack success criteria
- Physical specification has partial coverage — some dimensions or materials decisions are still open
- User experience specification covers the primary interaction but secondary interactions are undefined
- At least one visual reference is attached
- The Design Decision Log has been started but not all major decisions are recorded

**Ready to Move Forward:**
- Functional specification is complete: all core functions described with success criteria
- Physical specification defines form factor, materials, dimensions, and weight target
- User experience specification covers the primary user interaction end-to-end
- At least one visual reference is attached
- Open Design Questions list has been reviewed and each item categorized: resolve before prototyping or resolve during prototyping
- No critical design decisions remain undocumented

---

## Completion Criteria

- Functional specification is complete: all core product functions are defined with success criteria
- Physical specification defines form factor, materials, dimensions, and weight target
- User experience specification covers the primary user interaction from first encounter through completed use
- At least one visual reference — sketch, reference photo, or wireframe — is attached to the specification
- Design Decision Log is complete for all major decisions
- Open Design Questions list is finalized and each item categorized: resolve before prototyping or resolve during
- No critical design decision remains undocumented

---

## Handoff

The following information moves from Stage 5 into Stage 6 (Prototype):

- **Product Design Specification** — the primary input to the prototyper; the complete design document they will work from
- **Design Decision Log** — the reasoning behind every design choice; the prototyper needs this context to make judgment calls during the build
- **Open Design Questions** — becomes the prototype testing agenda; each question is a specific thing the prototype must answer
- **Visual references** — attached sketches, photos, and references carried forward as design intent artifacts
- **Specialist Engagement Recommendations** — any specialist engagements that must happen before or during prototyping are flagged for the inventor to act on
- **Materials and manufacturing decisions** — the specific materials and process choices from this stage feed Stage 7 (Manufacturing) for supplier identification

The Product Design Specification also carries forward to Stage 7 (Manufacturing) as the manufacturer-facing technical reference. Nothing from the design record is discarded.
